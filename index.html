About

A concise intellectual portrait with room to grow into a fuller academic biography.

My work is driven by questions at the boundary of text and voice, memory and inscription, metaphysics and historical process. I am particularly interested in how oral traditions preserve, transform, and authorize meaning across generations.

Much of my research centers on the Qurʾān as a lived recitational phenomenon rather than only as a stabilized written object. This interest connects naturally to broader philosophical questions about cognition, embodiment, communal memory, and the formation of interpretive traditions.

I also work on Islamic philosophy, early Shiʿi intellectual history, and the relationship between religion and politics in both classical and modern contexts. Across these fields, I am interested in how conceptual traditions remain intellectually alive through repetition, commentary, ritual, and disciplined study.

Primary Fields Philosophy, cognitive science, Islamic studies, intellectual history
Core Themes Orality, memory, recitation, transmission, hermeneutics
Methods Conceptual analysis, historical interpretation, comparative inquiry
Ongoing Aim Building bridges between philosophy of mind, oral tradition, and Islamic intellectual history

Research Themes

Selected areas that organize the broader arc of the site and can later become separate pages.

Qurʾānic Studies

Orality and Recitation

Investigating the Qurʾān through recitational practice, oral memory, and the emergence of variant readings, with attention to the dynamic relation between preservation and performance.

Cognitive Inquiry

Memory and Extended Mind

Exploring how repetition, embodiment, communal discipline, and musicality support durable memory systems, especially in traditions that rely on oral transmission.

Islamic Philosophy

Metaphysics and Intellectual History

Studying Islamic philosophical traditions and their conceptual developments, including questions of meaning, knowledge, being, and the transmission of wisdom across eras.

Shiʿi Studies

Early Shiʿi Discourses

Examining formative Shiʿi materials, doctrinal development, messianic themes, moral philosophy, and the role of transmitted reports in shaping intellectual identity.

Comparative Thought

Text, Voice, and Authority

Comparing how traditions negotiate the relationship between written fixation and living interpretation, especially where communal recitation sustains claims to authenticity.

Modernity and Ideological Formation
